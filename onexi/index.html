Introduction.


Boston is ranked amongst the 5 most expensive cities in the United States [1]. Real estate prices have risen by more than 100% in neighborhoods like Dorchester and Roxbury, and an estimate of rent index by Zillow shows that the cost of renting a 1-bedroom apartment in Boston is almost twice the national average [2]. As rents and prices keep increasing, the question of the hour is:

"Can we afford to live in Boston anymore?"

This project aims to understand how real estate valuations have been affected by the recession, and their steep increase in recent years. Using machine learning, we predict prices for the near future, and identify neighborhoods that are going to be affected the most. Our vision is to understand which neighborhoods require affordable housing interventions from the City of Boston.

Methodology.


Concept


The singular challenge of this project lies in the fact that parcels change shape and size over time. We transform parcel polygons to centroid points and use them for analysis. To that effect, we obtain a set of independent variables [Time (Year), Latitude (Centroid Y), Longitude (Centroid X)] while the dependent variable is assessed value of that parcel. This is a feasible proxy of real estate prices in that parcel. Our aim is to fit a function that approximates this curve in 4-dimensional space using two different machine learning techniques.

Machine Learning: Ridge Regression


Ridge regression is an extension of the linear regression model, where a parsimonious model is created as the number of predictor variables in a dataset exceeds the number of observations. While an ordinary least squares (OLS) regression seeks to minimize the sum of squared residuals, a regularization term (usually the L2 norm of the weights) is added to the objective function for ridge regression. We implemented this model using the scikit-learn library in Python.

Neighborhoods.


Boston's diverse neighborhoods serve as a political and cultural organizing mechanism. Although the City of Boston's Office of Neighborhood Services has designated 23 neighborhoods in the city [6], we use 26 neighborhoods as defined by the Metropolitan Area Planning Council (MAPC) [7]. It should be noted that as the city of Boston has grown and evolved, its neighborhoods have changed as well. We use the latest version (2018) of neighborhood geographical boundaries from MAPC.

See the interactive map on the right.



We make predictions for each parcel for each time period using the two machine learning models described above (ridge regression and neural network). Aggregating these parcel-level values to a neighborhood-level average, we can see how the true value and our predictions change over time for the 26 neighborhoods in Boston.

Note that there are only predicted values for 2018, 2019 and 2020 as true values are not available.

Parcels.


In real estate, a lot or plot is a tract or parcel of land owned or meant to be owned by some owner(s). Possible owner(s) of a lot can be one or more person(s) or another legal entity, such as a company/corporation, organization, government, or trust. Like most other types of real estate, lots owned by private parties are subject to a periodic real estate tax payable by the owners to local governments such as a county or municipality. These real estate taxes are based on the assessed value of the real property; additional taxes usually apply to transfer of ownership and property sales.


A lot has defined boundaries (or borders) which are documented, but the boundaries need not be shown on the land itself. Most lots are small enough to be mapped as if they are flat, in spite of the curvature of the earth. A characteristic of the size of a lot is its area. The area is typically determined as if the land is flat and level although the contour surface area of the land is changeable and may be too complicated for determining a lot's area.


Parcels change shape and size over time. For example, larger parcels may be broken down into multiple smaller parcels during development of that area. A classic case in Boston is that of parcels near the airport and East Boston waterfront.

Conclusion.


  • Linear regression (ridge regression) is not a good predictor of price trends.
  • Neural networks are very effective at capturing spatio-temporal trends. Even a relatively simple single-layer neural network (like ours) provided decent predictions.
  • Neighborhood spatial effects are important indicators of real estate prices.

Future research potential.


Enhancing the model is essential to getting better predictions, but that comes with the requirement of computational resources. We provide four ideas future research efforts.


  • A simple extension would be using a Support Vector Machine with non-linear kernels since it is clear that the regression problem is not linear.
  • Explore alternative methods, such as the kernel method, that leverage data being in an Euclidean space (latitude and longitude). Consider feature approximations of the kernels of interest.
  • Consider locally weighted regression, where models are fitted at query time, when data points are near to a particular query. This follows the intuition that points in Allston (on the other side of the city from the query point in East Boston) are unlikely to influence its value.
  • Recurrent neural networks (RNN), like the Long Short Term Memory (LSTM) network with convolutional layers, help to incorporate spatial structure. This is quite since the prices of a place in a year are dependent on their prices in previous years. As the rate of development, and hence the price values, varies from neighborhood to neighborhood, having different weights for spatial regions in the RNN is an appropriate way to proceed.


Additionally, obtaining more data to construct further features would greatly enhance the prediction capability. Having said that, we recognize that obtaining built-environment or demographic data in a annual time-series format might not always be feasible.
